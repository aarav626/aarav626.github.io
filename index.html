Investment Playbook

Learn what each metric means so the simulator isn‚Äôt a black box.

Asset Allocation

How you split your money across broad buckets like stocks, bonds, real estate, and alternatives. Research suggests this decision explains most of your long-run performance.

Volatility

The standard deviation of returns ‚Äì loosely, how ‚Äúbouncy‚Äù your portfolio is. High volatility isn‚Äôt bad by itself, but it makes drawdowns more likely.

Sharpe Ratio

Measures return per unit of risk. Two portfolios can have the same return, but the one with lower volatility will have a higher Sharpe.

Drawdown

The peak-to-trough fall in your portfolio. A ‚àí50% drawdown needs +100% just to break even ‚Äì which is why risk control matters.

Quick Glossary
  • Alpha ‚Äì excess return above a benchmark (e.g., S&P 500) after adjusting for risk.
  • Beta ‚Äì sensitivity to market moves. A beta of 1 moves with the market; 0.5 moves half as much.
  • Correlation ‚Äì how two assets move together. Low or negative correlation helps diversification.
  • Value at Risk (VaR) ‚Äì a probabilistic ‚Äúworst case‚Äù over a given time horizon at a chosen confidence level.
  • Dollar-Cost Averaging ‚Äì investing the same amount on a regular schedule, regardless of price.

Core Investing Principles

These foundational ideas drive all portfolio construction, risk modeling, and long-term wealth building. Understanding them will dramatically improve how you use this simulator.

1. Time in the Market

Compounding is the single most powerful force in investing. Even small returns grow exponentially when given enough time. Missing just a few strong market days can reduce long-run gains dramatically.

2. Diversification

No single asset wins every year. A diversified portfolio smooths returns by balancing high-growth assets with stabilizers like bonds, real estate, and alternatives.

3. Risk ‚â† Volatility

Volatility reflects price movement, not true risk. The real risk is permanent loss of capital or failing to meet long-term financial goals.

4. Market Cycles

Markets move in cycles ‚Äî expansion, peak, contraction, and recovery. Successful investors stay objective and avoid emotional reactions during downturns.

Advanced Investment Concepts

These concepts help explain how professional investors model return, risk, and correlations across asset classes.

Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT)

Developed by Harry Markowitz, MPT shows that a portfolio‚Äôs risk is not just the sum of its parts ‚Äî it depends on how the assets move relative to one another. Combining low-correlation assets creates a more efficient risk/return profile.

  • Efficient Frontier: The set of portfolios with the highest return for a given risk level.
  • Optimal Portfolio: The point where your risk tolerance intersects the frontier.
  • Correlation: Lower is better for diversification.

The Role of Bonds in a Portfolio

Bonds are often seen as ‚Äúboring,‚Äù but they serve as a volatility dampener and income stream. They typically perform well when equities struggle, providing stability during recessions.

Why Crypto Is High-Risk, High-Reward

Digital assets like Bitcoin and Ethereum operate in a rapidly evolving market. They offer outsized upside, but with rapid drawdowns and regulatory uncertainty. A small allocation can add meaningful convexity to a portfolio.

Extended Glossary

  • Compound Interest ‚Äì earnings that themselves earn earnings over time.
  • Market Capitalization ‚Äì the total value of a company or crypto asset.
  • Liquidity ‚Äì how easily an asset can be bought or sold.

  • Sharpe Ratio ‚Äì return per unit of risk.
